## New Drafts

### P1.M2 — ACT Composite Score
> Every North Carolina junior takes the ACT, giving families and educators a common measure of college readiness. Students who score higher open doors to more college choices and stronger career opportunities.

**Words:** 32 | **Research:** ACT Inc. (2012): ACT score predicts college completion; Mattern & Cruce (2021): higher scores linked to higher earnings by age 28

---

### P1.M3 — AP Participation
> Taking Advanced Placement courses challenges students with college-level work before they leave high school. Students who take on that challenge build skills and confidence that carry into college and careers.

**Words:** 31 | **Research:** College Board "Impacts of AP": even students scoring below 3 show better college outcomes than similar non-AP peers

---

### P1.M4 — AP Performance
> Passing an AP exam proves a student can master college-level material. Students who earn a passing score can receive college credit, saving their families time and tuition while getting a head start on a degree.

**Words:** 36 | **Research:** Smith, Hurwitz & Avery (2017, J. Labor Economics): each passing AP score causally increases bachelor's completion by 1-2 pp

---

### P1.M6 — Dual Enrollment Participation
> Dual enrollment lets students take real college courses while still in high school. Students who participate enroll in college at higher rates and arrive at college with momentum already behind them.

**Words:** 31 | **Research:** CCRC (2024): 81% of dual enrollment students enrolled in college after HS vs. ~70% overall; IES WWC (2017): positive effects on degree attainment

---

### P1.M7 — Dual Enrollment Credit
> Enrolling in a college course matters, but earning the credit proves a student can do the work. Students who finish with credit shorten their path to a degree and reduce tuition costs for their families.

**Words:** 37 | **Research:** NCES (2019): dual enrollment credits shorten time to degree and reduce tuition; CCRC (2024): completers earn degrees at higher rates

---

### P1.M8 — CTE Participation
> Career and Technical Education connects classroom learning to the workplace. Students who participate in CTE build practical skills and stay engaged in school, preparing them for jobs or further education.

**Words:** 30 | **Research:** ACTE (2025): CTE concentrators graduate at 96% vs. 85% nationally; CTE Research Network/AIR (2024): positive effects on achievement, completion, employability — no negative effects found

---

### P1.M9 — CTE Credentials
> Millions of good-paying jobs do not require a four-year degree. Students who earn industry credentials can show employers they have real-world skills — and that proof helps them land stronger jobs.

**Words:** 33 | **Research:** Georgetown CEW: 29M jobs pay $35K+ without bachelor's; Fordham Institute (2024): earning credentials in HS linked to positive employment outcomes

---

### P1.M11 — NAEP Grade 4 Reading
> Students who read well by fourth grade are far more likely to graduate. This is the moment when children shift from learning to read to reading to learn every other subject.

**Words:** 33 | **Research:** Annie E. Casey Foundation (2010): children not proficient by 3rd grade are 4x more likely to not graduate; Chall: "learning to read / reading to learn" transition at grades 3-4

---

### P1.M12 — NAEP Grade 8 Reading
> By eighth grade, reading is the tool students use to learn science, history, and every other subject. Strong readers enter high school ready to handle rigorous coursework across all classes.

**Words:** 32 | **Research:** IES/IRIS Center: by middle school, reading comprehension underpins learning in every content area

---

### P1.M13 — NAEP Grade 4 Math
> When children build strong math skills early, the advantage stays with them for years. Students who master the basics — like fractions, patterns, and problem-solving — carry that confidence through every grade that follows.

**Words:** 35 | **Research:** Duncan et al. (2007, Developmental Psychology): early math skills are the single strongest predictor of later academic achievement; Siegler et al. (2012): fractions knowledge predicts HS math achievement

---

### P1.M14 — NAEP Grade 8 Math
> Eighth-grade math determines whether students can access algebra, calculus, and other advanced courses. Students who master these skills keep the door open to college and STEM career opportunities.

**Words:** 31 | **Research:** Multiple sources: only ~2% of STEM majors who arrive at college needing precalculus earn a STEM degree; 8th-grade math proficiency is the gateway to the STEM pipeline

---

### P1.M15 — Schools of Character
> Schools that teach character alongside academics help students do better in class and in life. Students in these schools build habits like teamwork, resilience, and responsibility that support success beyond the classroom.

**Words:** 34 | **Research:** Jeynes (2019, Education and Urban Society): character education associated with higher achievement across 52 studies; Durlak et al. (2011, Child Development): SEL programs yield 11-percentile-point academic gain

---

### P8.M2 — Public School Enrollment
> Public schools serve every child regardless of income, ability, or background — providing meals, special education, and essential support services. Research shows students in diverse schools do better academically and build stronger connections.

**Words:** 31 | **Research:** NCES (2024): public schools serve 49.6M students, 91% participate in USDA meal programs, IDEA mandates services; Century Foundation (2016): diverse schools produce stronger academic outcomes
